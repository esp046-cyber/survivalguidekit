Water & Food Supplies

HIGH
üíä Water Purification
Tablets, drops, or portable filter. Never drink untreated water from natural sources. Boiling for 1 minute is most reliable.
HIGH
ü•´ Non-Perishable Food
Energy bars, dried fruits, nuts, and canned goods. Pack at least 72 hours worth of calories (2000+ per day).
MEDIUM
üé£ Fishing Line & Hooks
Compact fishing kit for catching food. Even basic supplies can provide protein in wilderness survival.
LOW
üçµ Metal Cup/Pot
For boiling water and cooking. Stainless steel is lightweight and durable.
MEDIUM
üßÇ Salt & Seasonings
Salt helps preserve food and maintain electrolytes. Small containers of spices improve morale with better-tasting meals.
LOW
üç´ Emergency Rations
High-calorie survival bars or MREs. Can last 5+ years. Each bar provides 400+ calories.
MEDIUM
ü•§ Electrolyte Balance
Powder packets for hydration. Critical in heat, diarrhea, vomiting. DIY: 6 tsp sugar, 1/2 tsp salt per liter water. Prevents dangerous electrolyte loss.
LOW
‚òï Energy Foods
Instant coffee/tea for morale, warmth. Honey never spoils, instant energy. Peanut butter high protein/fat. Hard candies boost morale, provide quick sugar.
üí° TIP: Look for water near valleys, green vegetation, and animal tracks. Morning dew can be collected with cloth. Never ration water if you have it‚Äîdrink what you need.
‚ö†Ô∏è WARNING: Avoid water with foam, strange odors, or dead animals nearby. Clear water isn't always safe‚Äîalways purify.
Water Purification Methods:
‚úì Boiling: 1 min at sea level, 3 min above 6,500ft
‚úì Tablets: Follow package directions, wait 30+ min
‚úì UV Light: Stir water while exposing for 90 sec
‚úì Filter: Clean filter regularly, test flow rate
‚úì Solar: Clear bottle in sun for 6+ hours kills bacteria
‚úì Cloth Filter: Pre-filter debris (not purification)
‚úì Charcoal: Activated carbon removes some chemicals
‚úì Emergency: Bleach (2 drops per quart, wait 30min)

Shelter & Fire Building

HIGH
üèïÔ∏è Emergency Blanket
Mylar thermal blanket reflects body heat and is waterproof. Can also signal aircraft with reflective surface.
HIGH
ü™¢ Paracord (50ft)
550 paracord has multiple uses: building shelter, making traps, fishing line, and emergency repairs.
MEDIUM
üé™ Tarp or Poncho
Waterproof covering for shelter construction. Can be worn as rain gear or used as ground cover.
MEDIUM
üî• Fire Tinder
Dryer lint, cotton balls with petroleum jelly, or commercial fire starters. Keeps moisture-free in waterproof bag.
LOW
‚õ∫ Sleeping Bag
Temperature-rated for your climate. Mummy-style retains heat better. Keep dry at all costs.
LOW
üõèÔ∏è Sleeping Pad
Insulates from cold ground. Inflatable or foam. Ground steals body heat faster than air.
MEDIUM
ü™® Rock Heat Storage
Heat rocks in fire, place in shelter for warmth (NOT river rocks - explode). Test temperature first. Wrap in fabric. Reheats all night. Avoid shale/porous rocks.
LOW
üå≤ Natural Insulation
Pine needles, leaves, grass for bedding (12+ inches thick). Creates air pockets. Stuff inside clothing for warmth. Change daily if damp. Debris hut principle.
üí° TIP: Build shelter before dark. Look for natural windbreaks. Keep fire triangle in mind: heat, fuel, oxygen. Build fires in safe cleared areas away from overhanging branches.
Fire Building Steps:
1. Gather tinder (dry grass, bark, paper)
2. Collect kindling (small twigs, pencil-size)
3. Prepare fuel wood (arm-thick branches)
4. Build structure (teepee or log cabin style)
5. Light tinder, shield from wind
6. Gradually add kindling, then fuel wood
7. Maintain with dry wood, monitor constantly
8. Build reflector wall to direct heat
‚ö†Ô∏è WARNING: Never leave fire unattended. Clear 10ft radius of debris. Keep water nearby. Fully extinguish before sleeping or leaving.

First Aid Essentials

HIGH
ü©π Bandages & Gauze
Assorted adhesive bandages, sterile gauze pads, and medical tape for wound care.
HIGH
üíä Basic Medications
Pain relievers, antihistamines, anti-diarrhea medication, and any personal prescriptions.
HIGH
üß¥ Antiseptic
Alcohol wipes, antiseptic cream, and hand sanitizer to prevent infection.
MEDIUM
‚úÇÔ∏è Trauma Supplies
Scissors, tweezers, safety pins, and a tourniquet for serious injuries.
LOW
üßä Cold/Heat Packs
Instant cold packs for sprains, swelling. Heat packs for hypothermia, muscle pain.
MEDIUM
üìã Medical Manual
Pocket first aid guide. Critical when no signal for internet. Covers CPR, fractures, bites.
LOW
üíâ Specialized Supplies
EpiPen for allergies, glucose tablets for diabetics, inhaler for asthma. Pack personal medical needs with expiration tracking.
MEDIUM
üßº Hygiene Supplies
Soap, toothbrush, toilet paper, feminine products. Clean hands prevent infection. Maintaining hygiene boosts morale and health.
LOW
ü©∫ CPR Knowledge
30 chest compressions, 2 rescue breaths. Push hard (2 inches), fast (100-120/min). Continue until help arrives or victim breathes. AED instructions if available.
MEDIUM
ü¶ü Insect Bite Treatment
Remove stinger (scrape, don't pinch). Clean area, ice for swelling. Baking soda paste reduces itching. Watch for allergic reactions. Tick removal: tweezers, pull straight up.
üí° TIP: Clean wounds immediately with clean water. Apply pressure to stop bleeding. Change bandages daily. Learn basic CPR and the Heimlich maneuver before emergencies occur.
Treating Common Injuries:
‚Ä¢ Cuts: Clean, apply pressure, elevate, bandage
‚Ä¢ Burns: Cool with water 10-20min, don't pop blisters
‚Ä¢ Sprains: Rest, Ice, Compress, Elevate (RICE)
‚Ä¢ Fractures: Immobilize, don't realign, seek help
‚Ä¢ Hypothermia: Remove wet clothes, warm gradually
‚Ä¢ Dehydration: Sip water slowly, add electrolytes
‚Ä¢ Blisters: Don't pop, cover with moleskin/bandage
‚Ä¢ Nosebleed: Lean forward, pinch soft part 10min
‚ö†Ô∏è WARNING: Shock can kill. Keep victim warm, calm, lying down with legs elevated. Never give unconscious person anything to drink.

Natural Navigation Tips:
‚Ä¢ Sun: East at sunrise, south at noon (N. Hemisphere)
‚Ä¢ Stars: North Star points north, find Big Dipper
‚Ä¢ Moon: Crescent tips point north (N. Hemisphere)
‚Ä¢ Stick Shadow: Mark shadow tip every 15min for E-W
‚Ä¢ Landmarks: Note prominent features, triangulate
‚Ä¢ Watch Method: Hour hand to sun, halfway to 12 = south
‚Ä¢ Ant Hills: Often face south (N. Hemisphere)
‚Ä¢ Tree Growth: Denser on south side (generally)
‚ö†Ô∏è WARNING: If lost, STOP - Sit, Think, Observe, Plan. Don't panic. Stay where you are unless in immediate danger. Make yourself visible.

‚Ä¢ Contour lines close together = steep terrain
‚Ä¢ V-shapes pointing uphill = valley/stream
‚Ä¢ Blue = water features (rivers, lakes)
‚Ä¢ Green = vegetation (forests, swamps)

Emergency Signals & Communication

HIGH
üì¢ SOS Signal
Universal distress: 3 short, 3 long, 3 short (... --- ...). Use whistle, light, smoke, or sound. Repeat every minute.
HIGH
üî• Signal Fire
Three fires in triangle (100ft apart) or straight line. Add green branches for white smoke during day.
MEDIUM
‚úàÔ∏è Ground-to-Air Signals
Make large symbols with rocks/logs. "X" = need help, "I" = need medical, "F" = need food/water, "‚Üí" = going this way.
MEDIUM
üì± Cell Phone Tips
Text uses less battery than calls. Turn off when no signal. Climb high ground for better reception. Keep in airplane mode to save power.
LOW
üé® Bright Markers
Orange/red fabric, emergency blankets. Tie to trees, lay on ground. Contrasts with natural colors, visible from air.
MEDIUM
üì¢ Sound Signals
Three of anything = SOS. Whistle: 3 blasts, rest, repeat. Banging pots, shouting. Sound travels far in valleys, gets absorbed in forest. Use regularly during day.
LOW
üí° Light Signals
Flashlight SOS: 3 short, 3 long, 3 short. Strobe mode if available. Visible for miles at night. Point at aircraft/vehicles. Save battery - signal only when needed.
üí° TIP: International ground signal: Wave both arms overhead in large, slow movements. One arm waving means "do not land/all okay."
Signal Priority Order:
1. Mirror flash (daytime, clear weather)
2. Whistle blasts (3 in a row, 1 minute apart)
3. Signal fire with smoke (add green vegetation)
4. Flares (nighttime or poor visibility)
5. Ground markers (make large, 12ft+ symbols)
‚ö†Ô∏è WARNING: Never fire gun into air for signal - dangerous and illegal in many areas. Use only approved signaling devices.
Making Yourself Findable:
‚Ä¢ Stay in open areas, avoid dense forest
‚Ä¢ Create contrast - bright colors vs surroundings
‚Ä¢ Make noise regularly during day
‚Ä¢ Leave trail markers if you must move
‚Ä¢ Keep fire smoking during day (green leaves)
‚Ä¢ Arrange rocks/logs into geometric patterns

Clothing & Protective Gear

HIGH
üëï Layering System
Base layer (moisture-wicking), mid layer (insulation), outer layer (wind/waterproof). Remove layers before sweating.
HIGH

Waterproof boots with ankle support. Break in before trips. Bring extra socks (wool or synthetic, never cotton).

Work gloves for tasks, warm gloves for cold. Keep hands dry. Mittens warmer than gloves in extreme cold.
MEDIUM

LOW

Waterproof jacket and pants. Staying dry is critical. Hypothermia can occur even above 50¬∞F if wet.
MEDIUM

LOW

Clothing Do's & Don'ts:
‚úì DO wear moisture-wicking base layers
‚úì DO dress in layers you can adjust
‚úì DO keep extra clothes in waterproof bag
‚úó DON'T wear cotton in cold/wet conditions
‚úó DON'T overdress - you'll sweat and chill
‚úó DON'T forget to protect extremities
‚ö†Ô∏è WARNING: Hypothermia symptoms: shivering, confusion, slurred speech, drowsiness. Re-warm slowly. Remove wet clothes immediately.
Clothing Maintenance:
‚Ä¢ Dry clothes by fire (not too close - melts synthetics)
‚Ä¢ Repair tears immediately with duct tape/sewing kit
‚Ä¢ Rotate socks daily, air out boots at night
‚Ä¢ Shake out clothing before wearing (insects/debris)
‚Ä¢ Store in waterproof bags when not wearing
‚Ä¢ Use spare clothes as pillow/insulation at night

Wildlife Safety & Awareness

HIGH
üêª Bear Encounters
Make noise while hiking. If spotted: stay calm, don't run, appear large, back away slowly. Bear spray effective 30ft range.
HIGH
üêç Snake Safety
Watch where you step/sit. Most bites occur when stepping on or handling snakes. If bitten: stay calm, immobilize limb, seek help immediately.
MEDIUM
ü¶ü Insect Protection
DEET or picaridin repellent. Tuck pants into socks. Check for ticks daily. Mosquitoes spread diseases - use netting in endemic areas.
MEDIUM
üçñ Food Storage
Hang food 12ft high, 6ft from trunk, 100+ yards from camp. Never sleep with food. Store toiletries with food - animals smell it all.
LOW
ü¶ù Small Animals
Raccoons, squirrels carry diseases. Don't feed or approach. Secure all food and trash. Rabies risk - avoid all wildlife contact.
MEDIUM
üï∑Ô∏è Dangerous Insects
Black widow, brown recluse spiders. Scorpions in desert. Check shoes/clothing before wearing. Shake out sleeping bag. Most active at night. Bites need medical attention.
LOW
ü¶Ö Birds of Prey
Rarely attack humans. If nesting: give wide berth. Cover head if swooping. Back away slowly. Don't look up directly. Most defend nest only, won't pursue.
üí° TIP: "If it's black, fight back. If it's brown, lie down. If it's white, goodnight." (Black bears: be aggressive. Grizzly: play dead. Polar: extremely dangerous.)
Wildlife Encounter Protocol:
1. Stay calm, assess the situation
2. Never run - triggers chase instinct
3. Make yourself large, make noise
4. Back away slowly while facing animal
5. If attacked, fight back or play dead (species dependent)
6. Report all dangerous encounters to authorities
‚ö†Ô∏è WARNING: Never approach wildlife, especially with young. Maintain 100+ yards from bears/wolves, 25+ yards from other large animals. Fed animals become dangerous.
Animal Behavior Indicators:
‚Ä¢ Ears back, teeth showing = aggressive, back away
‚Ä¢ Bluff charges (stops short) = testing you, stand ground
‚Ä¢ Circling = predatory behavior, very dangerous
‚Ä¢ Chattering/hissing = warning, respect space
‚Ä¢ Fresh tracks/scat = animal nearby, increase caution
‚Ä¢ Carcass remains = predator territory, leave area

Weather & Climate Awareness

HIGH
‚õàÔ∏è Storm Recognition
Dark clouds, sudden wind change, temperature drop indicate storms. Seek shelter immediately. Lightning: count seconds between flash and thunder, divide by 5 for miles away.
HIGH
‚ùÑÔ∏è Cold Weather
Wind chill can be deadly. Exposed skin freezes in minutes below 0¬∞F. Layer clothing, stay dry, keep moving. Watch for frostbite on extremities.
HIGH
‚òÄÔ∏è Heat Safety
Heat exhaustion symptoms: heavy sweating, weakness, nausea. Heat stroke is life-threatening: hot dry skin, confusion, unconsciousness. Cool victim immediately.
MEDIUM
üåä Flash Floods
Never camp in dry riverbeds or valleys. Water can rise within minutes from distant rain. Move to high ground immediately if flooding occurs.
MEDIUM
üå´Ô∏è Fog & Visibility
Reduce travel in poor visibility. Use compass, don't trust sense of direction. Mark trail, take frequent bearings. Sound carries differently in fog.
LOW
üí® Wind Assessment
Strong winds increase fire danger, hypothermia risk, make shelters difficult. Seek protected areas. Wind chill dramatically lowers effective temperature.
MEDIUM
üåä Water Level Awareness
Flash floods from distant rain. Never camp in washes, valleys, low ground. Water rises in minutes. Move to high ground at first sign. Currents extremely dangerous.
LOW
‚òÅÔ∏è Cloud Formations
Cirrus (wispy) = change coming. Cumulus (puffy) = fair weather. Cumulonimbus (towering dark) = thunderstorm imminent. Mammatus clouds = severe weather, take shelter immediately.
üí° TIP: Red sky at night = sailor's delight. Red sky at morning = sailor's warning. Ring around moon = rain within 24hrs. These old sayings have meteorological basis.
Reading Weather Signs:
‚Ä¢ High thin clouds ‚Üí weather changing in 24hrs
‚Ä¢ Clouds lowering/darkening ‚Üí rain approaching
‚Ä¢ Wind from south/southeast ‚Üí storm likely
‚Ä¢ Pressure drop (headache/joint pain) ‚Üí bad weather
‚Ä¢ Animals seeking shelter ‚Üí storm imminent
‚Ä¢ Morning dew absent ‚Üí rain possible
‚ö†Ô∏è WARNING: Lightning: Get off peaks/ridges immediately. Avoid lone trees, water, metal. Crouch low on insulated pad if caught in open. If hair stands up, lightning about to strike - drop immediately.

Essential Knots & Cordage

HIGH
ü™¢ Bowline Knot
Creates fixed loop that won't slip. "Rabbit comes out of hole, around tree, back down hole." Essential for rescue, securing loads, climbing.
HIGH
üîó Square Knot
Joins two ropes of equal diameter. "Right over left, left over right." NOT for climbing - can slip under load. Good for bandages, packages.
HIGH
‚öì Clove Hitch
Quick way to attach rope to post/tree. Easy to adjust. Two half hitches around object. Perfect for starting lashings on shelters.
MEDIUM
üéØ Taut-Line Hitch
Adjustable loop, slides to tighten/loosen. Essential for tent guy-lines and adjustable anchor points. Grips under tension, slides when slack.
MEDIUM
üîÑ Sheet Bend
Joins ropes of different diameters. Stronger than square knot. Double sheet bend for extra security on slippery rope or critical loads.
LOW
üé£ Improved Clinch
Fishing knot for tying hook to line. 95% strength retention. Thread through eye 5-7 times, loop back through, tighten slowly.
LOW
ü™¢ Prussik Knot
Friction hitch that grips when weighted, slides when not. Emergency ascending/descending rope. Use smaller diameter cord around larger rope.
üí° TIP: Practice knots at home until muscle memory develops. Wet rope before tightening critical knots. Always leave 6+ inch tail. Test every knot under load before trusting life to it.
Knot-Tying Best Practices:
‚úì Learn 5 essential knots thoroughly vs 20 poorly
‚úì Practice with eyes closed for night scenarios
‚úì Inspect knots before and during use
‚úì Use correct knot for task - wrong knot can kill
‚úì Backup critical knots with additional half hitch
‚úì Cut frayed or damaged rope - don't risk failure
‚úì Wet rope before tightening for strength
‚úì Leave 6+ inch tail on all knots
‚ö†Ô∏è WARNING: Never use square knot for climbing or life support - it can slip. Bowline can work loose with repeated loading/unloading - use backup knot or stopper.

Food Sources & Foraging Safety

HIGH
üçÑ Universal Edibility Test
NEVER eat unknown plants/mushrooms without testing. Rub on skin (15min), lips (3min), tongue (15min), chew/spit (15min), swallow small amount (5hrs). If no reaction, eat small portion.
HIGH
üêõ Insects as Protein
Remove wings/legs, cook thoroughly. Avoid bright colors, hairy/spiny, strong smells. Grasshoppers, crickets, ants (remove head), grubs are safe. High protein, 6-leg insects generally safe.
MEDIUM
üåø Safe Wild Plants
Dandelion (all parts), cattail (roots/shoots), clover, plantain, chickweed. Learn 5-10 local edibles thoroughly. Avoid milky sap (except dandelion), umbrella-shaped flowers, three-leafed plants.
MEDIUM
üé£ Fishing Techniques
Set multiple hooks on trot line. Fish at dawn/dusk. Look for structure (rocks, logs). Small fish for bait, catch bigger fish. Spear fish in shallow water by pinning to bottom.
MEDIUM
ü™§ Trapping Basics
Simple snare: wire loop on game trail. Deadfall trap: heavy log triggered by bait stick. Check traps twice daily. Legal/ethical only in true survival situations.
LOW
ü•ú Nuts & Seeds
Acorns (boil to remove tannins), pine nuts, walnuts. High calorie, stores well. Crack open, inspect for mold/insects before eating.
LOW
üçá Berries
General rule: Blue/black usually safe, red maybe, white/yellow avoid. Aggregate berries (raspberry/blackberry) are safe. Single berry on stem often poisonous.
MEDIUM
ü¶û Aquatic Food
Crayfish in streams (flip rocks). Frogs, turtles edible (cook thoroughly). Mussels/clams only from moving water. Avoid red tide periods. All require cooking to kill parasites.
LOW
üåæ Wild Grains
Cattail roots, wild rice, amaranth. Labor intensive but high carbs. Grind or pound to make flour. Cook to improve digestibility. Learn local varieties before emergency.
üí° TIP: Calories from foraging often less than energy spent finding food. In short-term survival, existing fat reserves sustain you. Focus on water first, food secondary.
Foraging Safety Rules:
1. NEVER eat mushrooms unless 100% certain
2. Avoid plants with milky/colored sap
3. Skip plants with umbrella flower clusters
4. Don't eat anything with almond/bitter smell
5. Avoid three-leafed plants (often poison)
6. Cook all animal protein to kill parasites
7. Eat only small amounts of new foods first
‚ö†Ô∏è WARNING: Starvation takes 3+ weeks. Poisoning takes hours. When in doubt, go without. One person should test new food, others wait 24hrs before eating.
Edible vs Poisonous Quick Check:
‚úì SAFE: Aggregate berries (raspberry type)
‚úì SAFE: Plants with square stems (mint family)
‚úì SAFE: Single seed in middle (stone fruits)
‚úó AVOID: White/yellow berries (95% poisonous)
‚úó AVOID: Milky sap (except dandelion)
‚úó AVOID: Umbrella-shaped flower clusters
‚úó AVOID: Almond/bitter smell
‚úó AVOID: Three-leafed plants

Mental Survival & Psychology

HIGH
üß† Survival Mindset
Will to live is #1 factor. Positive mental attitude (PMA) essential. Think, plan, act. Focus on what you can control. Small victories build confidence.
HIGH
üò∞ Managing Panic
STOP: Sit, Think, Observe, Plan. Deep breathing (4 count in, 4 hold, 4 out). Focus on immediate task, not entire situation. Panic wastes energy and causes mistakes.
HIGH
üéØ Prioritization
Survival priorities: Safety, Shelter, Water, Fire, Food, Rescue. Address immediate threats first. Break large problems into small, manageable tasks.
MEDIUM
üí™ Maintaining Morale
Keep busy with productive tasks. Maintain hygiene and routines. Talk to yourself positively. Recall happy memories. Humor helps - laugh when possible.
MEDIUM
üò¥ Sleep Management
Rest critical for decision-making. Sleep when safe to do so. Exhaustion leads to poor judgment. Power naps (20min) restore alertness without grogginess.
MEDIUM
üë• Group Dynamics
Assign roles, rotate leadership. Make decisions by consensus when possible. Address conflicts immediately. Lone survivors: talk aloud, stay connected to humanity.
LOW
üìù Journaling
Record events, thoughts, lessons learned. Organizes thinking, tracks progress. Useful for rescue debriefing. Provides mental outlet for stress.
üí° TIP: 10-80-10 Rule: 10% of people stay calm in crisis, 80% become confused/stunned, 10% panic. Training moves you to the 10% who act effectively.
Building Mental Resilience:
‚Ä¢ Visualize success before attempting tasks
‚Ä¢ Celebrate small wins - fire started, shelter built
‚Ä¢ Maintain daily routines when possible
‚Ä¢ Stay physically active - movement fights despair
‚Ä¢ Think about loved ones - strong motivation
‚Ä¢ Practice gratitude - focus on what you have
‚Ä¢ Set short-term achievable goals daily
‚Ä¢ Talk to yourself positively and out loud
‚Ä¢ Sing songs, tell stories - maintain humanity

Hunting & Tracking Skills

HIGH
üêæ Track Identification
Fresh tracks have sharp edges, filled tracks are older. Measure stride length, look for claw marks. Deer: heart-shaped, no claws. Canine: oval with claws. Cat: round, no claws, retractable.
HIGH
ü¶å Reading Animal Signs
Scat indicates diet and species. Rubs on trees (deer marking territory). Browse lines show feeding height. Fresh digging means recent activity. Follow trails to water sources.
MEDIUM
üéØ Primitive Weapons
Spear: straight sapling, fire-hardened point. Sling: effective 50+ yards with practice. Throwing stick: good for small game. Bow requires weeks of practice - not for beginners in emergency.
MEDIUM
ü™§ Snare Construction
Wire loop on game trail, 4 fingers wide for rabbits. Anchor securely. Check twice daily. Spring snare lifts animal off ground. Legal only in survival situations. Remove after use.
MEDIUM
üêøÔ∏è Small Game Hunting
Rabbits, squirrels easier than large game. Hunt dawn/dusk when active. Still-hunt: move slowly, pause frequently. Look for movement, not whole animal. Aim for vital organs.
LOW
üî™ Field Dressing
Hang animal head-down. Cut around anus, careful not to puncture intestines. Open belly sternum to pelvis. Remove organs, save heart/liver. Cool meat quickly. Cook thoroughly.
LOW
üßÇ Meat Preservation
Smoke over fire (not too hot). Salt heavily if available. Slice thin, dry in sun. Pemmican: dried meat powder mixed with fat. Keep flies away during drying process.
üí° TIP: In survival, fishing and trapping more reliable than hunting. Hunting burns calories you may not replace. Set multiple traps, check regularly. One meal not worth days of stalking.
Tracking Fundamentals:
‚Ä¢ Look for tracks near mud, sand, soft ground
‚Ä¢ Morning light shows tracks better (shadows)
‚Ä¢ Follow disturbed vegetation, broken twigs
‚Ä¢ Wider spacing = animal running/scared
‚Ä¢ Deep impressions = heavy animal or soft ground
‚Ä¢ Circle track site to find direction of travel
‚ö†Ô∏è WARNING: Never approach wounded game - extremely dangerous. Cook all wild meat thoroughly to kill parasites. Avoid brain/spinal tissue (disease risk). Wash hands after handling raw meat.

Medicinal Plants & Natural Remedies

HIGH
üåø Plantain (Common)
Leaves treat insect bites, cuts, stings. Chew into poultice, apply to wound. Draw out infection/venom. Grows in lawns, trails. Parallel veins, oval leaves.
HIGH
üåº Yarrow
Stops bleeding, antiseptic. Crush leaves into wound. Tea for fever/colds. White/pink flower clusters, feathery leaves. Common in fields, roadsides.
MEDIUM
üçÉ Willow Bark
Natural aspirin (salicin). Chew inner bark or make tea for pain/fever. Don't use if allergic to aspirin. Found near water. Young bark more potent.
MEDIUM
üå∫ Pine Tree
Pine needle tea high in Vitamin C (prevents scurvy). Sap is antiseptic, seals wounds. Inner bark edible. Avoid ponderosa pine (toxic to livestock, may harm humans).
MEDIUM
üî• Aloe Vera
Burn treatment, skin irritation relief. Break leaf, apply clear gel directly. Reduces inflammation, speeds healing. Store broken leaves in cool place for later use.
LOW
üåæ Cattail
Gel from base treats burns, wounds. Young shoots edible. Fluff for tinder, insulation. Roots starchy (cook like potato). Found in marshes, pond edges.
LOW
üçÇ Oak Bark
Tannins are astringent. Tea treats diarrhea, sore throat. Gargle for mouth infections. Boil bark 10 minutes. Don't drink too much (constipation).
üí° TIP: Positive identification is CRITICAL. One plant family can have edible and deadly species. When uncertain, don't use. Learn 3-5 local medicinal plants thoroughly before emergency.

‚Ä¢ Yarrow: bleeding, infections, fever
‚Ä¢ Willow bark: pain, inflammation, fever

Vehicle & Stranded Survival

HIGH
üöó Stay With Vehicle
Vehicle is shelter, visible to rescuers, contains resources. Easier to spot than person. Run engine 10 min/hour for heat (crack window for ventilation). Clear exhaust of snow.
HIGH
üîã Battery Conservation
Turn off everything unnecessary. Use hazard lights only when traffic approaches. Interior light drains battery fast. Jump start may not work in extreme cold - keep battery warm.
HIGH
üé® Visibility Enhancement
Tie bright fabric to antenna. Spell HELP with rocks/branches. Open hood as distress signal. Write SOS in snow/dirt (large letters). Use mirrors/chrome for signaling.
MEDIUM
üî• Vehicle Heat Sources
Seat covers for insulation. Floor mats block drafts. Stuff newspapers in clothing for warmth. NEVER use candles/heaters inside (carbon monoxide). Huddle together for body heat.
MEDIUM
‚õΩ Fuel Management
Keep tank above half always. Siphon gas if needed (tube, gravity). Spare container in trunk. Gas stabilizer for long-term storage. In cold, add antifreeze to fuel line.
MEDIUM
üõ†Ô∏è Vehicle Resources
Tire jack for signaling (reflect sun). Seat belt cutters/window breakers. Oil for fire starting. Antifreeze is toxic - never drink. Spare tire rim makes fire ring.
LOW
üì± Communication
Text uses less power than calls. Turn phone off when no signal (searching drains battery). Car charger essential. Keep phone warm (battery dies in cold). Enable location services.
üí° TIP: Vehicle emergency kit: blankets, water, high-energy snacks, first aid, flashlight, jumper cables, shovel, sand/kitty litter (traction), flares, basic tools, duct tape.
Stuck in Snow/Mud Procedure:
1. Don't spin wheels - digs deeper
2. Straighten wheels, clear obstacles
3. Place mats/branches under drive wheels
4. Rock vehicle gently (forward/reverse)
5. Deflate tires slightly for more traction
6. Call for help if unable to free vehicle
‚ö†Ô∏è WARNING: Carbon monoxide kills. NEVER run engine with blocked exhaust. Check tailpipe every 30 minutes in snow. Crack window when engine running. Drowsiness is first symptom - exit immediately.

Cold Weather & Winter Survival

HIGH
‚ùÑÔ∏è Hypothermia Recognition
Stages: Shivering (mild), confusion/fumbling (moderate), no shivering/unconscious (severe). Rewarm gradually. Give warm drinks if conscious. Never give alcohol. Get to shelter immediately.
HIGH
üßä Frostbite Prevention
Cover all skin. White/gray/hard skin is frostbitten. Rewarm gradually in warm water (not hot). Don't rub - causes damage. Fingers, toes, nose, ears most vulnerable. Painful when thawing.
HIGH
üèîÔ∏è Snow Shelters
Quinzhee: pile snow, hollow out after settling 2hrs, 2ft thick walls. Snow cave: dig into drift, small entrance, shelf inside above entrance (cold air sinks). Poke vent hole in roof.
MEDIUM
üíß Winter Hydration
Melt snow before drinking (hypothermia risk eating). Yellow/pink snow unsafe. Dehydration common - cold air is dry. Need same water as summer. Warm drinks reduce heat loss.
MEDIUM
üî• Fire in Snow
Build platform of green logs (prevents sinking). Feather sticks for tinder. Dead standing wood drier than ground wood. Resin-rich wood burns hot. Reflector wall directs heat to shelter.
MEDIUM
‚ö° Winter Hazards
Ice: test before crossing, look for dark spots. Avalanche: avoid slopes 30-45¬∞, loud noises can trigger. Tree wells: deep holes around tree bases (suffocation risk). Whiteout: zero visibility.
LOW
üéø Snow Travel
Snowshoes spread weight (prevent sinking). Travel in valleys, avoid ridges (wind). Mark trail every 50ft. Travel during warmest part of day. Rest frequently to avoid sweating.
üí° TIP: "VIP" for layering: Ventilation (prevent sweating), Insulation (trap air), Protection (wind/waterproof). Adjust layers BEFORE sweating - wet clothes deadly in cold.
Cold Weather Priorities:
1. Find/build shelter immediately (wind chill kills)
2. Get fire started before dark
3. Insulate from ground (cold rises from below)
4. Stay dry at all costs (wet = hypothermia)
5. Cover head, extremities (major heat loss)
6. Keep moving but don't sweat
7. Eat high-fat foods (body fuel for heat)
‚ö†Ô∏è WARNING: Wind chill: -20¬∞F with 20mph wind feels like -48¬∞F. Exposed skin freezes in 10 minutes. Severe hypothermia victim may appear dead but can be revived - keep trying CPR until help arrives.

Desert Survival Strategies

HIGH
‚òÄÔ∏è Heat Management
Travel at night/early morning. Rest in shade during peak heat (10am-4pm). Light-colored, loose clothing reflects heat. Cover all skin to prevent sunburn and moisture loss.
HIGH
üíß Water Conservation
Breathe through nose (mouth loses moisture). Don't talk unnecessarily. Avoid alcohol/caffeine. Sip, don't gulp. Need 1 gallon per hour in extreme heat. Urine should be light yellow.
HIGH
üèúÔ∏è Desert Water Sources
Follow animal tracks at dawn/dusk. Look in rock crevices after rain. Dig in dry riverbeds (lowest point). Barrel cactus contains moisture (squeeze pulp). Bird flight patterns point to water.
MEDIUM
üåµ Desert Shelter
Dig below sand surface (cooler). Use tarp/blanket for shade. Natural caves/overhangs. Stay off ground (heat radiates up). Build shade before searching for water.
MEDIUM
ü¶Ç Desert Hazards
Shake out boots/clothes (scorpions, spiders). Never put hands where you can't see. Rattlesnakes in shade under rocks. Flash floods in canyons. Sandstorms: cover mouth/nose, seek shelter.
LOW
üî• Desert Fire
Dead cactus skeleton burns hot. Dried animal dung for fuel. Fire not essential unless cold nights. Smoke visible for miles - good signal. Reflective surface better than fire for daytime signal.
LOW
üß≠ Desert Navigation
Mark trail with rocks (arrows). Mirages distort distance. Stars reliable at night. Follow dry washes downhill (leads to civilization). Travel along ridges when possible (better view).
üí° TIP: Desert temperature swings 40¬∞F+ between day/night. Hypothermia risk at night. Layer clothing. Cold desert nights can be as dangerous as hot days.
Desert Survival Priorities:
1. Find shade immediately - heatstroke kills in hours
2. Ration sweat, not water - drink when thirsty
3. Cover skin completely - prevents moisture loss
4. Rest during heat, travel during cool hours
5. Signal location - wide open visibility
6. Watch for weather changes - flash floods deadly
‚ö†Ô∏è WARNING: Heat exhaustion ‚Üí heat stroke rapid. Symptoms: hot dry skin, confusion, seizures. Cool victim immediately - life threatening. Don't eat without water (digestion uses water). Desert kills through dehydration.

Jungle & Tropical Survival

HIGH
üíß Water Abundance
Rain collection easiest method. Vines contain water (cut high, then low, catch drip). Bamboo stalks hold water in segments. Always purify - parasites everywhere despite clear appearance.
HIGH
ü¶ü Disease Prevention
Mosquitoes carry malaria, dengue. Cover skin at dawn/dusk. Smoke from fire repels insects. Tuck pants into boots (leeches). Check body twice daily for ticks, leeches. Infections spread rapidly in humidity.
HIGH
üêç Jungle Hazards
Snakes in trees and ground. Use stick to probe ahead. Avoid tall grass. Centipedes, spiders deadly. Fire ants swarm. Never swim in unknown water (crocodiles, piranha, parasites, electric eel).
MEDIUM
üèïÔ∏è Elevated Shelter
Build off ground (floods, insects, animals). Use large leaves for waterproof roof. Bamboo/branches for platform. Steep roof angle for rain runoff. Clear ground below shelter.
MEDIUM
üî• Wet Environment Fire
Standing dead wood drier than ground wood. Bamboo explodes - vent before burning. Rubber/latex from trees burns even wet. Keep tinder dry in waterproof container. Fire platform raises above wet ground.
MEDIUM
üå¥ Tropical Food
Coconuts: green for water, brown for meat. Banana/plantain always cooked. Palm hearts edible. Avoid bright-colored fruits. Most tropical plants require cooking. Learn local edibles before travel.
LOW
üß≠ Jungle Navigation
Follow water downstream (leads to civilization). Cut trail markers every 20 feet. Compass essential (dense canopy blocks sun). Mark camp clearly. Easy to get disoriented in dense vegetation.
üí° TIP: Jungle rich in resources but also dangers. Stay dry to prevent fungal infections. Change socks daily. Air out feet. Wet clothes rot skin. Keep wounds clean and covered.
Tropical Environment Challenges:
‚Ä¢ High humidity prevents sweat evaporation
‚Ä¢ Constant moisture rots gear and clothes
‚Ä¢ Dense vegetation makes travel exhausting
‚Ä¢ Insects and diseases prevalent
‚Ä¢ Poor visibility - easy to get lost
‚Ä¢ Flash floods common in rainy season
‚Ä¢ Everything requires more energy in heat/humidity
‚ö†Ô∏è WARNING: Never drink from stagnant water (parasites). Avoid all brightly colored animals (poison). Don't touch unknown plants (skin irritants). Machete essential but dangerous - always cut away from body.

Coastal & Open Water Survival

HIGH
üåä Ocean Water Danger
NEVER drink seawater - accelerates dehydration, kidney failure. Causes madness and death. Need to desalinate: solar still, distillation. Collect rain water aggressively. Fish body fluids emergency only.
HIGH
üé£ Ocean Food Sources
Fish near floating debris. Avoid brightly colored fish (toxic). Spear fish in shallows. Seaweed edible (rinse freshwater). Shellfish from rocks (check for red tide). Cook all seafood thoroughly.
HIGH
ü¶à Marine Hazards
Sharks attracted to blood, splashing. Jellyfish stings (vinegar/urine treatment). Stonefish camouflaged (deadly). Box jellyfish lethal. Sea snakes venomous. Riptides pull you out (swim parallel to shore).
MEDIUM
üèñÔ∏è Beach Shelter
Above high tide line (check debris). Natural shade from cliffs, palm trees. Driftwood for structure. Avoid coconut trees directly (falling coconuts deadly). Wind protection essential.
MEDIUM
üí® Weather Awareness
Storms develop quickly over water. Tide cycles (6 hours rise/fall). Storm surge floods low areas. Lightning strikes water. Tsunamis after earthquakes - move to high ground immediately.
MEDIUM
üî• Signal Fire
Beach visible from distance - good for signals. Three fires in triangle. Add green vegetation for smoke. Rubber/oil creates black smoke (daytime). Keep fire going continuously if possible.
LOW
üö§ Raft Survival
Stay with craft unless sinking. Ration water strictly (1 pint/day minimum). Shade from sun (sunburn accelerates dehydration). Collect dew at night. Fish attracted to shade under raft.
üí° TIP: Salt water rots wounds and causes infections. Rinse cuts with fresh water if available. Keep dry when possible. Sun reflection off water intensifies - double sunburn risk.
Coastal Resource Gathering:
‚Ä¢ Low tide exposes tide pools (shellfish, crabs)
‚Ä¢ Seaweed abundant protein/vitamins (rinse well)
‚Ä¢ Birds indicate fish schools offshore
‚Ä¢ Driftwood for fire, shelter construction
‚Ä¢ Beach plastic/debris for containers, tools
‚Ä¢ Sand can filter water (pre-treatment only)
‚ö†Ô∏è WARNING: Hypothermia in water 20x faster than air. 50¬∞F water can kill in 1 hour. Get out of water ASAP. Remove wet clothes, get dry. Never swim at night (disorientation, predators).

Mountain & High Altitude Survival

HIGH
‚õ∞Ô∏è Altitude Sickness
Symptoms above 8,000ft: headache, nausea, fatigue, confusion. Descend immediately if severe. Acclimatize slowly. Drink extra water. High altitude cerebral/pulmonary edema deadly - emergency descent required.
HIGH
‚ùÑÔ∏è Temperature Extremes
Temperature drops 3-5¬∞F per 1,000ft elevation. Windchill severe on exposed ridges. Frostbite risk even above freezing with wind. Layer clothing, protect extremities. Hypothermia kills quickly at altitude.
HIGH
‚òÄÔ∏è Sun Intensity
UV radiation 10-12% stronger per 3,000ft. Snow reflection doubles exposure (snow blindness). Severe sunburn in hours. Cover all skin. Sunglasses essential. Lips crack and bleed - use lip balm.
MEDIUM
ü™® Terrain Hazards
Loose rocks, scree slopes. Test footing before weight transfer. Avalanche danger on slopes 30-45¬∞. Rock falls common. Stay away from cliff edges. Lightning strikes peaks - descend during storms.
MEDIUM
üíß Mountain Water
Streams above tree line usually clean but purify anyway. Melt snow for water (energy intensive). Dehydration common in dry air. Drink before thirsty. Need extra at altitude (breathing moisture loss).
MEDIUM
üèîÔ∏è Mountain Shelter
Seek wind protection (rocks, trees). Below tree line safer than exposed ridge. Cave/overhang ideal. Insulate from ground. Build windbreak with rocks/snow. Small shelter retains heat better.
LOW
‚¨áÔ∏è Safe Descent
Stay on ridges when possible (visible). Follow water downhill. Avoid steep descents (injury risk). Three points of contact on rough terrain. Descending conserves energy versus climbing.
üí° TIP: "Climb high, sleep low" - acclimatize by day, descend to sleep. Weather changes rapidly in mountains. Storm can hit in minutes. Always have escape route planned.
Mountain Environment Factors:
‚Ä¢ Thinner air = less oxygen (fatigue, poor decisions)
‚Ä¢ Faster dehydration in dry mountain air
‚Ä¢ Weather unpredictable - prepare for worst
‚Ä¢ Longer recovery time from exertion at altitude
‚Ä¢ Fire difficult - less oxygen for combustion
‚Ä¢ Sound travels differently - distance deceptive
‚Ä¢ Cooking takes longer (lower boiling point)
‚ö†Ô∏è WARNING: Never continue up with altitude sickness symptoms - only cure is descent. HACE/HAPE can kill in 12-24 hours. Most mountain deaths from poor decisions caused by hypoxia (oxygen deprivation affecting judgment).
